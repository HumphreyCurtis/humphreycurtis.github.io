Research "Computer Says No" — CHI 2026 · Prompting Change — ASSETS 2026

People with aphasia are routinely excluded from the digital welfare systems they depend on. Benefits portals, housing applications, healthcare booking systems, GP apps — these services assume a level of literacy, digital fluency and communicative competence that many stroke survivors simply do not have. The consequences are severe: lost entitlements, failed appeals, financial hardship, and the emotional cost of navigating hostile bureaucracy without adequate support.

This is not an abstract problem. One co-designer — P4, living with aphasia — attempted to migrate his Universal Credit claim by phone. The call began with automated robocalls that repeatedly misinterpreted his speech, requiring him to verbally spell his name, phone number and answer six security questions. He was placed on extended hold and instructed to "go online" despite his aphasia. When his call dropped unexpectedly at 6pm, he was told to restart the entire process the next day. He ultimately had to attend the Job Centre in person — undermining the very premise of digital access.

Advokit is a response to this — a generative AI-enhanced digital toolkit co-designed with stroke survivors with aphasia, speech and language therapists and welfare advisors at Aphasia Re-Connect. The design process is documented in two peer-reviewed papers presented at CHI 2026 and submitted to ASSETS 2026.

Community Web Toolkit

Advokit is live at advokit.uk. I designed and built the toolkit end-to-end — full-stack architecture, accessibility infrastructure, the LLM-powered Benefit Buddy assistant and the community stories pipeline. Every feature was directly shaped by co-design findings: the traffic-light difficulty ratings, the progressive disclosure of application tips, the read-aloud function with word-level highlighting, and the structured feedback controls in Benefit Buddy all trace back to specific barriers named by co-designers across the four stages. The five sections below document each component of the toolkit.

Toolkit Overview

Advokit comprises five core sections: a homepage, an about page, a benefits list, a comparative benefits table and a stories page. The homepage introduces the project through a short overview video, followed by four illustrated tiles supporting common user pathways: considering whether to apply for welfare, having already started an application, exploring real welfare stories from people with aphasia and learning more about the project. Short explainer videos are embedded throughout the platform to enhance accessibility and support independent navigation.

Benefits List and Table

The benefits list page presents the 21 welfare benefits currently supported by Advokit, complemented by a sortable comparative table for side-by-side comparison and prioritisation. The decision to present benefits as a navigable list and comparative table — rather than a linear document or static guide — arose directly from co-design sessions in which participants described feeling overwhelmed by the volume and complexity of available benefits and uncertain about where to begin. Perceived application difficulty and value emerged as the most salient factors shaping decisions about which benefits to pursue. Difficulty is communicated using a traffic-light metaphor — easy (green), medium (amber) and hard (red) — while value is represented through a five-star rating, supporting rapid, low-demand comparison for people with aphasia.

Benefit Pages

Individual benefit pages were designed to minimise textual density, reflecting the reading support needs common among people with aphasia. Key information is presented succinctly, with additional detail available through interactive drop-down sections and related links. All textual content includes a read-aloud function with word-level highlighting. Each page includes community-generated application tips — contributed by welfare veterans, caregivers and co-designers — presented using a progressive disclosure design that allows users to reveal them incrementally or view them all at once. All tip lists and resource collections are printable to support offline access.

Benefit Stories

The stories page hosts YouTube-embedded interviews with community welfare veterans from Stage 2, accompanied by commissioned illustrations, captions and printable accessible transcripts. This section foregrounds lived experience as a shared community resource, enabling visitors to engage with welfare journeys through multimodal narratives. Each story highlights the individual's experiences with specific benefits, a brief synopsis, their top application tips and personal reflections. By combining video, illustration and text, this section supports varied literacy levels and interaction preferences.

Benefit Buddy

Benefit Buddy is an LLM-powered conversational assistant designed to support benefit applications for people with aphasia. Unlike general-purpose chatbots, it is specifically configured to scaffold welfare application writing: supporting collaborative text ideation, breaking complex questions into smaller steps and coaching applicants to articulate the functional impacts of their condition in the language assessors require. At the onset of each interaction, Benefit Buddy presents structured onboarding questions to establish context — the benefit being applied for, the current claim stage and relevant health conditions. All chat messages can be read aloud using integrated text-to-speech and copied directly into application forms. Structured feedback buttons — Good response, Bad response, More detail and Less detail — allow iterative refinement without additional typing.

Co-Design Process

The toolkit was developed across four stages from late May to late July 2025, involving 8 workshops, 7 interviews and over 60 participants across SLT centres, community venues and online.

Stage 2 workshops used accessible card decks — Help cards explaining benefit types in plain language, Where cards listing access channels, Barrier cards naming common obstacles, and Feelings cards — to help co-designers rank and articulate their experiences of welfare services. The resulting value/difficulty grids surfaced what mattered most: being understood, getting help quickly, not having to repeat yourself.

Stage 3 used journey mapping toolkits — a squiggly-line format representing the emotional arc of a welfare journey — to trace the specific moments where things went wrong. Co-designers physically placed Where and Barrier cards along the map, annotating with their own words. The resulting maps were dense with compound failures: inaccessible forms, phone queues, confusing letters, staff who didn't know about aphasia.

Stage 4 used speculative design fiction: short video prompts depicting near-future AI welfare scenarios — an automated eligibility denial, an AI meeting bot, a document camera, an inaccessible medical form — to ground discussion in vivid possibility rather than abstract hypotheticals. Co-designers responded with their own visions for what empathetic, accessible AI welfare services could look like.

Open-Source Workshop Toolkit

A core contribution of Advokit is a fully open-source co-design toolkit that enables others to run their own workshops with disabled communities on digital welfare access. It includes accessible card decks, printable facilitation materials and five speculative design fiction video prompts. Download and use freely.
