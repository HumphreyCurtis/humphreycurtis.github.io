Zuzenna's Diary

Zuzenna is a stroke survivor living with aphasia. Over four months, she maintained a self-made communication diary — a bespoke, handcrafted AAC tool built not from any clinical template or commercial product, but from her own imagination, materials and needs. The diary grew organically: photographs, cut-outs, hand-drawn symbols, ticket stubs, pressed flowers, personal memorabilia. It was entirely on her own terms.

This project is a qualitative study of that diary — of what it tells us about what AAC can and should be. We worked alongside Zuzenna to understand how her diary functioned: as a communication tool, yes, but also as an expression of identity, a record of life after stroke and a deeply personal artefact. The study challenges the dominant model of high-tech, medicalised AAC and argues instead for designs that are shareable, agentive and capable of supporting personal growth.

The research asks what happens when you step back and let someone with aphasia lead their own design process. The answer, in Zuzenna's case, is something unexpectedly beautiful — and generative for the field. The paper was presented at ASSETS 2025 in Denver and received broad engagement from the accessibility research community.
